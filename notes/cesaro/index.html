Converse

It is natural to ask whether there exists a converse to Cesàro's lemma: if the averages of a sequence converge, does the sequence itself converge? This cannot be true without further asumptions: taking un=(−1)nu_{n} = (-1)^{n}un​=(−1)n, the Cesàro averages converge to 000, but (un)(u_{n})(un​) does not converge.

In the case of real-valued sequences, a simple converse is the following.

Proposition Suppose (un)(u_{n})(un​) is a monotonous, real-valued sequence such that its averages converge. Then (un)(u_{n})(un​) converges to the same limit as its averages:

lim⁡n→∞un=lim⁡n→∞1n∑k=1nuk.\lim_{n \to \infty} u_{n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k = 1}^{n} u_{k}.n→∞lim​un​=n→∞lim​n1​k=1∑n​uk​.

Proof By the monotone convergence theorem for real-valued sequences, we infer that (un)(u_{n})(un​) converges to some ℓ∈R‾\ell \in \overline{\mathbb{ R}}ℓ∈R. By Cesàro's lemma, the averages of (un)(u_{n})(un​) also converge to ℓ\ellℓ. ■\blacksquare■

With a stronger assumption, one can show the following converse, known as the weak version of Hardy's theorem. Morally, if the averages of a sequence converge and the successive terms of the sequence accumulate increasingly fast, then the sequence converges.

Application

Cesàro's lemma is very useful when applied to telescoping sequences. If (un)(u_{n})(un​) converges to 000, it is sometimes easy to show that the sequence (vn)(v_{n})(vn​) defined by vn≐un+1α−unαv_{n} \doteq u_{n+1}^{\alpha} - u_{n}^{\alpha}vn​≐un+1α​−unα​ converges to a finite and non-zero limit ℓ\ellℓ for some appropriate choice of α<0\alpha < 0α<0. Cesàro's lemma then provides

1n(unα−u0α)=1n∑k=0n−1vk→ℓ, \frac{1}{n} (u_{n}^{\alpha} - u_{0}^{\alpha}) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k = 0}^{n - 1} v_{k} \to \ell, n1​(unα​−u0α​)=n1​k=0∑n−1​vk​→ℓ,

which shows that unα∼ℓnu_{n}^{\alpha} \sim \ell nunα​∼ℓn; that is, un∼(ℓn)1/αu_{n} \sim (\ell n)^{1/\alpha}un​∼(ℓn)1/α. Importantly, Cesaro's lemma is used in the method of small steps, see this note.

More generally, if there exists a function f:R→Rf: \mathbb{ R} \to \mathbb{ R}f:R→R such that vn≐f(un+1)−f(un)v_{n} \doteq f(u_{n+1}) - f(u_{n})vn​≐f(un+1​)−f(un​) converges to ℓ∈R\{0}\ell \in \mathbb{ R} \backslash \{0\}ℓ∈R\{0}, then the same method shows f(un)/n∼ℓf(u_{n}) / n \sim \ellf(un​)/n∼ℓ.

If (un)(u_{n})(un​) is positive and un+1/un→ℓu_{n+1} / u_{n} \to \ellun+1​/un​→ℓ, then taking f=ln⁡f = \lnf=ln shows ln⁡(un)∼ln⁡(ℓ)n\ln(u_{n}) \sim \ln(\ell) nln(un​)∼ln(ℓ)n, therefore ln⁡(un)/n∼ln⁡(ℓ)\ln(u_{n}) / n \sim \ln(\ell)ln(un​)/n∼ln(ℓ). Composing by the exponential, we reach un1/n∼ℓu_{n}^{1/n} \sim \ellun1/n​∼ℓ.
