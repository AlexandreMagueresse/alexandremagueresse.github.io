There is more to say about maps that are continuous everywhere.

Uniform continuity

In the definition of continuity, δ\deltaδ is allowed to depend on xxx and ε\varepsilonε. The following stronger definition of continuity requires that δ\deltaδ be independent of xxx.

Definition (Uniform continuity) A map f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y is uniformly continuous if for all ε>0\varepsilon > 0ε>0, there exists δ>0\delta > 0δ>0 such that fff sends any ball of radius δ\deltaδ in XXX into a ball of radius ε\varepsilonε in YYY. More precisely, dY(f(x),f(y))≤εd_{Y}(f(x), f(y)) \leq \varepsilondY​(f(x),f(y))≤ε for all x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X such that dX(x,y)≤δd_{X}(x, y) \leq \deltadX​(x,y)≤δ.

The set of uniformly continuous functions from XXX to YYY is denoted U(X,Y)\mathcal{ U}(X, Y)U(X,Y).

Example From the previous examples, looking at the dependence of δ\deltaδ on ε\varepsilonε, we observe that the identity map, the constant map, the distance map and the power function with α∈[0,1]\alpha \in [0, 1]α∈[0,1] are uniformly continuous. However, the power function with α>1\alpha > 1α>1 is not uniformly continuous.

It is useful to characterise the dependence of δ\deltaδ on ε\varepsilonε for a uniformly continuous function. This is what the modulus of continuity measures. We first identify candidate moduli of continuity.

Definition (Modulus of continuity) A function ω:R+→R+\omega: \mathbb{ R}_{+} \to \mathbb{ R}_{+}ω:R+​→R+​ is a modulus of continuity if ω\omegaω is non-decreasing, ω(0)=0\omega(0) = 0ω(0)=0 and ω\omegaω is continuous at 000.

The set of moduli of continuity is written MMM. Note that MMM is stable under non-negative linear combinations and composition. We can now characterise uniform continuity with moduli of continuity.
