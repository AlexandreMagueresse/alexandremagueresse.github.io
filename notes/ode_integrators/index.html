General framework

We consider the initial value problem

u(t0)=u0,r(t,u,u˙)=0,\boldsymbol{ u}(t_{0}) = \boldsymbol{ u}_{0}, \qquad \boldsymbol{ r}(t, \boldsymbol{ u}, \dot{\boldsymbol{ u}}) = \boldsymbol{ 0},u(t0​)=u0​,r(t,u,u˙)=0,

where

  • u:R→Rn\boldsymbol{ u}: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^{n}u:R→Rn is the unknown of the problem,

  • t0∈Rt_{0} \in \mathbb{R}t0​∈R is the initial time and u0∈Rn\boldsymbol{ u}_{0} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}u0​∈Rn is the initial condition,

  • r:R×Rn×Rn→Rn\boldsymbol{ r}: \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}^{n} \times \mathbb{R}^{n} \to \mathbb{R}^{n}r:R×Rn×Rn→Rn is a discretised residual operator.

We are willing to evolve u\boldsymbol{ u}u until tF>t0t_{F} > t_{0}tF​>t0​. We split the interval [t0,tF]\left[ t_{0}, t_{F}\right][t0​,tF​] into sub-intervals [tn,tn+1]\left[ t_{n}, t_{n+1}\right][tn​,tn+1​] and evolve the solution from tnt_{n}tn​ to tn+1t_{n+1}tn+1​ iteratively. More formally, we consider the following general framework:

  • The encoder map E:Rn→S\mathcal{ E}: \mathbb{R}^{n} \to \mathcal{ S}E:Rn→S embeds the initial condition into a state space S\mathcal{ S}S,

  • The update map U:R+×S→S\mathcal{ U}: \mathbb{R}_{+} \times \mathcal{ S} \to \mathcal{ S}U:R+​×S→S evolves the state during one time interval [tn,tn+1]\left[ t_{n}, t_{n+1}\right][tn​,tn+1​],

  • The decoder map D:S→Rn\mathcal{ D}: \mathcal{ S} \to \mathbb{R}^{n}D:S→Rn converts the state into the value of u\boldsymbol{ u}u.

We then consider the recurrence

s0=E(u0),sn+1=U(hn,sn),un=D(sn),\boldsymbol{ s}_{0} = \mathcal{ E}(\boldsymbol{ u}_{0}), \qquad \boldsymbol{ s}_{n+1} = \mathcal{ U}(h_{n}, \boldsymbol{ s}_{n}), \qquad \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} = \mathcal{ D}(\boldsymbol{ s}_{n}),s0​=E(u0​),sn+1​=U(hn​,sn​),un​=D(sn​),

where hn=tn+1−tnh_{n} = t_{n+1} - t_{n}hn​=tn+1​−tn​. We want to design (E,U,D)(\mathcal{ E}, \mathcal{ U}, \mathcal{ D})(E,U,D) so that un\boldsymbol{ u}_{n}un​ is a good approximation of u(tn)\boldsymbol{ u}(t_{n})u(tn​). In particular, note that at time t0t_{0}t0​ we must have D∘E(u0)=u0\mathcal{ D} \circ \mathcal{ E}(\boldsymbol{ u}_{0}) = \boldsymbol{ u}_{0}D∘E(u0​)=u0​; in other words, D∘E=Id⁡Rn\mathcal{ D} \circ \mathcal{ E} = \operatorname{Id}_{\mathbb{R}^{n}}D∘E=IdRn​.

A scheme is consistent if the local truncation error goes to zero with the step size, where the local truncation error is defined as un+1−u(tn+1)\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{ u}(t_{n+1})un+1​−u(tn+1​) assuming that un=u(tn)\boldsymbol{ u}_{n} = \boldsymbol{ u}(t_{n})un​=u(tn​). A scheme has order ppp if un+1−u(tn+1)=O(hnp+1)\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{ u}(t_{n+1}) = \mathcal{ O}(h_{n}^{p+1})un+1​−u(tn+1​)=O(hnp+1​).

The region of absolute stability is the set of all complex z=λhz = \lambda hz=λh such that when evolving the linear ODE r(t,u,u˙)=u˙−λu\boldsymbol{ r}(t, \boldsymbol{ u}, \dot{\boldsymbol{ u}}) = \dot{\boldsymbol{ u}} - \lambda \boldsymbol{ u}r(t,u,u˙)=u˙−λu, the numerical solution tends to 0\boldsymbol{ 0}0 when time goes to infinity. A scheme is A-stable if its region of absolute stability contains the entire left complex plane. A scheme is L-stable if it is A-stable and if the rate at which un\boldsymbol{ u}_{n}un​ goes to 0\boldsymbol{ 0}0 increases as ℜ(λ)→−∞\Re(\lambda) \to -\inftyℜ(λ)→−∞. In the case of a linear scheme, one can identify an amplification matrix A(z):S→S\mathcal{ A}(z): \mathcal{ S} \to \mathcal{ S}A(z):S→S. The stability of the scheme directly depends on the eigenvalues of A(z)\mathcal{ A}(z)A(z).

θ\thetaθ-method

The θ\thetaθ method consists in enforcing a zero residual at tn+θ=(1−θ)tn+θtn+1t_{n + \theta} = (1 - \theta) t_{n} + \theta t_{n+1}tn+θ​=(1−θ)tn​+θtn+1​. More precisely, find v∈Rn\boldsymbol{ v} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}v∈Rn such that

r(tn+θ,un+θhnv,v)=0\boldsymbol{ r}(t_{n+\theta}, \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} + \theta h_{n} \boldsymbol{ v}, \boldsymbol{ v}) = \boldsymbol{ 0}r(tn+θ​,un​+θhn​v,v)=0

and set un+1=un+hnv\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1} = \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} + h_{n} \boldsymbol{ v}un+1​=un​+hn​v. The third-order Taylor expansion of the exact solution is

Observe that regardless of θ\thetaθ, the expansions u(tn+1)\boldsymbol{ u}(t_{n+1})u(tn+1​) and un+1\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1}un+1​ coincide up to O(hn)\mathcal{ O}(h_{n})O(hn​), so the θ\thetaθ-method is at least of order 111. In the case of a linear first-order ODE, one readily finds v=λ(1−θz)−1un\boldsymbol{ v} = \lambda (1 - \theta z)^{-1} \boldsymbol{ u}_{n}v=λ(1−θz)−1un​, and

A(z)=1+z1−θz.\mathcal{ A}(z) = 1 + \frac{z}{1 - \theta z}.A(z)=1+1−θzz​.

Observe that

∣A(z)∣≤1 ⟺ (1−2θ)∣z∣2+2ℜ(z)≤0.\left| \mathcal{ A}(z)\right| \leq 1 \iff (1 - 2 \theta) \left| z\right|^{2} + 2 \Re(z) \leq 0.∣A(z)∣≤1⟺(1−2θ)∣z∣2+2ℜ(z)≤0.

We distinguish three classes:

  • If θ<1/2\theta < 1/2θ<1/2, the stability region is the circle of radius 1/(1−2θ)1 / (1 - 2 \theta)1/(1−2θ) centred at (−1/(1−2θ),0)(-1/(1 - 2 \theta), 0)(−1/(1−2θ),0). This scheme is not AAA-stable. The case θ=0\theta = 0θ=0 is known as Forward Euler and it is the only explicit scheme of the family.

  • If θ=1/2\theta = 1/2θ=1/2, the stability region is the whole left complex plane. The scheme is AAA-stable. It is known as the implicit midpoint scheme.

  • If θ>1/2\theta > 1/2θ>1/2, the stability region includes the whole complex plane except the circle of radius 1/(2θ−1)1 / (2 \theta - 1)1/(2θ−1) and centred at (1/(2θ−1),0)(1 /(2 \theta - 1), 0)(1/(2θ−1),0). In particular, this scheme is AAA-stable. The special case θ=1\theta = 1θ=1 is known as Backward Euler.

The scheme is only LLL-stable when θ=1\theta = 1θ=1.

Runge-Kutta

The Runge-Kutta method is a multi-stage method consisting in enforcing a zero residual at intermediate times between tnt_{n}tn​ and tn+1t_{n+1}tn+1​, and forming un+1\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1}un+1​ from linear combinations of these intermediate solutions. More precisely, given s≥1s \geq 1s≥1, c∈Rs\boldsymbol{ c} \in \mathbb{R}^{s}c∈Rs, A∈Rs×s\boldsymbol{ A} \in \mathbb{R}^{s \times s}A∈Rs×s and b∈Rs\boldsymbol{ b} \in \mathbb{R}^{s}b∈Rs, find (v1,…,vs)(\boldsymbol{ v}_{1}, \ldots, \boldsymbol{ v}_{s})(v1​,…,vs​) such that

r(tn+cihn,un+hn∑j=1sAijvj,vi)=0\boldsymbol{ r}(t_{n} + \boldsymbol{ c}_{i} h_{n}, \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} + h_{n} \sum_{j = 1}^{s} \boldsymbol{ A}_{ij} \boldsymbol{ v}_{j}, \boldsymbol{ v}_{i}) = \boldsymbol{ 0}r(tn​+ci​hn​,un​+hn​j=1∑s​Aij​vj​,vi​)=0

for all i∈{1,…,s}i \in \{1, \ldots, s\}i∈{1,…,s} and finally set

un+1=un+hn∑i=1sbivi.\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1} = \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} + h_{n} \sum_{i = 1}^{s} \boldsymbol{ b}_{i} \boldsymbol{ v}_{i}.un+1​=un​+hn​i=1∑s​bi​vi​.

A Runge-Kutta is thus entirely determined from the coefficients A\boldsymbol{ A}A, b\boldsymbol{ b}b and c\boldsymbol{ c}c. They are conveniently arranged in the Butcher tableau format

Applied to a linear ODE with constant step size hhh, letting v=(v1,…,vs)∈Rs\boldsymbol{ v} = (\boldsymbol{ v}_{1}, \ldots, \boldsymbol{ v}_{s}) \in \mathbb{R}^{s}v=(v1​,…,vs​)∈Rs, this recursion becomes

v=λ(1sun+hAv),un+1=un+hb∗v.\boldsymbol{ v} = \lambda (\boldsymbol{ 1}_{s} \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} + h \boldsymbol{ A} \boldsymbol{ v}), \qquad \boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1} = \boldsymbol{ u}_{n} + h \boldsymbol{ b}^{\ast} \boldsymbol{ v}.v=λ(1s​un​+hAv),un+1​=un​+hb∗v.

We infer v=λ(I−zA)−11sun\boldsymbol{ v} = \lambda (\boldsymbol{ I} - z \boldsymbol{ A})^{-1} \boldsymbol{ 1}_{s} \boldsymbol{ u}_{n}v=λ(I−zA)−11s​un​ and un+1=(1+zb∗(I−zA)−11)un\boldsymbol{ u}_{n+1} = (1 + z \boldsymbol{ b}^{\ast} (\boldsymbol{ I} - z \boldsymbol{ A})^{-1} \boldsymbol{ 1}) \boldsymbol{ u}_{n}un+1​=(1+zb∗(I−zA)−11)un​, that is

In the following, we look at one- and two-stage schemes and satisfy the order conditions one by one until possible.

1 stage

A generic one-stage, first-order RK scheme looks like

αα1,\begin{array}{c|c} \alpha & \alpha \\ \hline & 1 \end{array},α​α1​​,

for some α∈R\alpha \in \mathbb{R}α∈R (usually α∈[0,1]\alpha \in [0, 1]α∈[0,1]). This is precisely the θ\thetaθ-scheme presented above.

This scheme has order 222 when α=1/2\alpha = 1/2α=1/2; it is the implicit midpoint rule corresponding to θ=1/2\theta = 1/2θ=1/2 in the θ\thetaθ-scheme. Neither of the conditions for order 333 are satisfied.
