General framework

Let f∈C(R×Rd,Rd)\boldsymbol{ f} \in C(\mathbb{ R} \times \mathbb{ R}^{d}, \mathbb{ R}^{d})f∈C(R×Rd,Rd) be continuous in the first variable and Lipschitz continuous in the second variable. For all p∈Rd\boldsymbol{ p} \in \mathbb{ R}^{d}p∈Rd, the Picard–Lindelöf theorem ensures the existence of an open interval I⊂RI \subset \mathbb{ R}I⊂R containing 000 and a unique u:I→Rd\boldsymbol{ u}: I \to \mathbb{ R}^{d}u:I→Rd such that

∀t∈I,u˙(t)=f(t,u(t)),u(0)=p.\forall t \in I, \quad \dot{\boldsymbol{ u}}(t) = \boldsymbol{ f}(t, \boldsymbol{ u}(t)), \qquad \boldsymbol{ u}(0) = \boldsymbol{ p}.∀t∈I,u˙(t)=f(t,u(t)),u(0)=p.

Besides, u\boldsymbol{ u}u depends continuously on u\boldsymbol{ u}u; in other words, this initial value problem is well-posed.

We are willing to approximate the flow of f\boldsymbol{ f}f in the interval [0,T][0, T][0,T] for some T∈I∩R+T \in I \cap \mathbb{ R}_{+}T∈I∩R+​. For simplicity, we construct an approximate flow using constant time steps. Let n≥1n \geq 1n≥1, define h=T/nh = T / nh=T/n and tk=kht_{k} = k htk​=kh for k∈{0,…,n}k \in \{0, \ldots, n\}k∈{0,…,n}.   • A marching map Mh:S→S\mathsf{ M}_{h}: \mathbb{ S} \to \mathbb{ S}Mh​:S→S evolves the state for one time step,

  • A finishing map Fh:S→Rd\mathsf{ F}_{h}: \mathbb{ S} \to \mathbb{ R}^{d}Fh​:S→Rd converts the state space into the solution space.

General linear methods

Runge-Kutta and linear multi-step methods can be examined under the broad class of general linear methods (GLM). In this class of methods, we allow for r≥1r \geq 1r≥1 reused steps and s≥1s \geq 1s≥1 internal stages. Runge-Kutta methods are recovered for r=1r = 1r=1, while linear multi-step methods correspond to s=1s = 1s=1. GLM were introduced to offer more flexibility than RK and LM methods, provide a better understanding of order conditions, and break order barriers.

The state space is thus S=(Rd)r\mathbb{ S} = (\mathbb{ R}^{d})^{r}S=(Rd)r. The starting and finishing procedures depend on the particular scheme; we describe them below. The marching procedure has the form

Mh(s)i=Vijsj+hBijf(wj),\mathsf{ M}_{h}(\boldsymbol{ s})_{i} = \boldsymbol{ V}_{ij} \boldsymbol{ s}_{j} + h \boldsymbol{ B}_{ij} \boldsymbol{ f}(\boldsymbol{ w}_{j}),Mh​(s)i​=Vij​sj​+hBij​f(wj​),

where w1,…,ws∈Rd\boldsymbol{ w}_{1}, \ldots, \boldsymbol{ w}_{s} \in \mathbb{ R}^{d}w1​,…,ws​∈Rd are defined implicitly via

wi=Uijsj+hAijf(Wj).\boldsymbol{ w}_{i} = \boldsymbol{ U}_{ij} \boldsymbol{ s}_{j} + h \boldsymbol{ A}_{ij} \boldsymbol{ f}(\boldsymbol{ W}_{j}).wi​=Uij​sj​+hAij​f(Wj​).

These schemes are characterised by the matrices A∈Rs×s\boldsymbol{ A} \in \mathbb{ R}^{s \times s}A∈Rs×s, B∈Rr×s\boldsymbol{ B} \in \mathbb{ R}^{r \times s}B∈Rr×s, U∈Rs×r\boldsymbol{ U} \in \mathbb{ R}^{s \times r}U∈Rs×r and V∈Rr×r\boldsymbol{ V} \in \mathbb{ R}^{r \times r}V∈Rr×r, as well as assumptions on the interpretation of the state variables, the shape of the stage variables and the finishing procedure. One usually assume that the starting procedure is such that the state at time ttt represents approximations of u(t)\boldsymbol{ u}(t)u(t) involving higher-order derivatives, that is

si(t)=∑k=0pα0,ku(t)+O(hp+1).\boldsymbol{ s}_{i}(t) = \sum_{k = 0}^{p} \boldsymbol{ \alpha}_{0, k} \boldsymbol{ u}(t) + O(h^{p+1}).si​(t)=k=0∑p​α0,k​u(t)+O(hp+1).

where α0,…,αp∈Rr\boldsymbol{ \alpha}_{0}, \ldots, \boldsymbol{ \alpha}_{p} \in \mathbb{ R}^{r}α0​,…,αp​∈Rr. We do not focus on the design of the starting procedure for now. The internal stages are assumed to represent

wi(t)=u(t+cih)+O(h2)\boldsymbol{ w}_{i}(t) = \boldsymbol{ u}(t + \boldsymbol{ c}_{i} h) + O(h^{2})wi​(t)=u(t+ci​h)+O(h2)

for some c∈Rs\boldsymbol{ c} \in \mathbb{ R}^{s}c∈Rs. Finally, we assume that the finishing procedure is just a linear combination of the state vectors, that is

F(s1,…,sr)=∑i=1rβisi,\mathsf{ F}(\boldsymbol{ s}_{1}, \ldots, \boldsymbol{ s}_{r}) = \sum_{i = 1}^{r} \boldsymbol{ \beta}_{i} \boldsymbol{ s}_{i},F(s1​,…,sr​)=i=1∑r​βi​si​,

for some β∈Rr\boldsymbol{ \beta} \in \mathbb{ R}^{r}β∈Rr.

Linear stability

Applied to the linear ODE f(t,u)=λu\boldsymbol{ f}(t, \boldsymbol{ u}) = \lambda \boldsymbol{ u}f(t,u)=λu, the recursions defining w\boldsymbol{ w}w and sk\boldsymbol{ s}_{k}sk​ read

w=Usk+zAw,sk+1=Vsk+zBw.\boldsymbol{ w} = \boldsymbol{ U} \boldsymbol{ s}_{k} + z \boldsymbol{ A} \boldsymbol{ w}, \qquad \boldsymbol{ s}_{k+1} = \boldsymbol{ V} \boldsymbol{ s}_{k} + z \boldsymbol{ B} \boldsymbol{ w}.w=Usk​+zAw,sk+1​=Vsk​+zBw.

We readily infer sk+1=(V+zB(I−zA)−1U)sk\boldsymbol{ s}_{k+1} = (\boldsymbol{ V} + z \boldsymbol{ B} (\boldsymbol{ I} - z \boldsymbol{ A})^{-1} \boldsymbol{ U}) \boldsymbol{ s}_{k}sk+1​=(V+zB(I−zA)−1U)sk​, and thus

uk=Fh∘(V+zB(I−zA)−1U)k∘Sh(p).\boldsymbol{ u}_{k} = \mathsf{ F}_{h} \circ (\boldsymbol{ V} + z \boldsymbol{ B} (\boldsymbol{ I} - z \boldsymbol{ A})^{-1} \boldsymbol{ U})^{k} \circ \mathsf{ S}_{h}(\boldsymbol{ p}).uk​=Fh​∘(V+zB(I−zA)−1U)k∘Sh​(p).

Strictly speaking, the linear stability analysis requires the knowledge of Sh\mathsf{ S}_{h}Sh​ and Fh\mathsf{ F}_{h}Fh​, but one usually directly relates it to the stability in the state space, and introduces

M(z)=V+zB(I−zA)−1U.\boldsymbol{ M}(z) = \boldsymbol{ V} + z \boldsymbol{ B} (\boldsymbol{ I} - z \boldsymbol{ A})^{-1} \boldsymbol{ U}.M(z)=V+zB(I−zA)−1U.

Zero-stability, pre-consistency and consistency

Applying a GLM to f=0\boldsymbol{ f} = \boldsymbol{ 0}f=0, we obtain

uk=Fh∘Vk∘Sh(p).\boldsymbol{ u}_{k} = \mathsf{ F}_{h} \circ \boldsymbol{ V}^{k} \circ \mathsf{ S}_{h}(\boldsymbol{ p}).uk​=Fh​∘Vk∘Sh​(p).

In particular for k=0k = 0k=0, one must have Fh∘Sh=Id⁡Rd\mathsf{ F}_{h} \circ \mathsf{ S}_{h} = \operatorname{Id}_{\mathbb{ R}^{d}}Fh​∘Sh​=IdRd​, so Sh\mathsf{ S}_{h}Sh​ must be left-invertible and Fh\mathsf{ F}_{h}Fh​ has to be a left-inverse of Sh\mathsf{ S}_{h}Sh​. It is often of interest to look for the pre-consistency and consistency at the stage level. The analysis of GLM is simplified through the formalism of B-series. We introduce ξ∈Bs\boldsymbol{ \xi} \in \mathsf{ B}^{s}ξ∈Bs to represent the stage vectors (corresponding to u(t+ch)\boldsymbol{ u}(t + \boldsymbol{ c} h)u(t+ch)) and η∈Br\boldsymbol{ \eta} \in \mathsf{ B}^{r}η∈Br for the state vectors (corresponding to αku(k)(t)\boldsymbol{ \alpha}_{k} \boldsymbol{ u}^{(k)}(t)αk​u(k)(t)). ξ=Uη+AξD=Ec+Oq+1,Eη=Vη+BξD+Op+1,E=β∗Eη+Oo+1.\boldsymbol{ \xi} = \boldsymbol{ U} \boldsymbol{ \eta} + \boldsymbol{ A} \boldsymbol{ \xi} \mathsf{ D} = \mathsf{ E}_{\boldsymbol{ c}} + \mathsf{ O}_{q+1}, \qquad \mathsf{ E} \boldsymbol{ \eta} = \boldsymbol{ V} \boldsymbol{ \eta} + \boldsymbol{ B} \boldsymbol{ \xi} \mathsf{ D} + \mathsf{ O}_{p+1}, \qquad \mathsf{ E} = \boldsymbol{ \beta}^{\ast} \mathsf{ E} \boldsymbol{ \eta} + \mathsf{ O}_{o+1}.ξ=Uη+AξD=Ec​+Oq+1​,Eη=Vη+BξD+Op+1​,E=β∗Eη+Oo+1​.
